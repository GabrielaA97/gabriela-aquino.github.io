Nowcasting GDP in a Small Open Economy: Mixed-Frequency Econometric and Machine Learning Evidence from El Salvador
2025
This study develops a unified nowcasting framework for El Salvador's quarterly GDP growth, evaluating seven models from three methodological families (classical time series, mixed-frequency econometric, and machine learning) across a harmonized panel of 123 indicators covering 2005Q1 to 2024Q2, with three publication-lag vintages and an 18-quarter rolling-origin out-of-sample test window. The central finding is a regime-dependent reversal of the model ranking: the Dynamic Factor Model dominates during the COVID crisis, while sparse linear and tree-based methods dominate during normal periods. Diebold-Mariano tests with the Harvey-Leybourne-Newbold small-sample correction confirm the inversion at the 5% significance level. In density forecasting, however, the DFM maintains its lead in both regimes, a finding with direct implications for uncertainty quantification in small open economies.

An Approach to the Measurement of Cross-Border Digital Trade in El Salvador
Un enfoque para la medición del comercio digital transfronterizo en El Salvador
Working Paper · Central Reserve Bank of El Salvador · Presented at UNCTAD E-Commerce Working Group, Geneva
Co-authored methodological paper documenting the use of 21 million annual credit and debit card transactions to produce El Salvador's first official statistics on cross-border digital trade. The methodology maps transaction-level data to standard balance-of-payments categories, generating quantitative estimates of digital platforms' contribution to GDP and the trade balance. Findings show that more than 70% of online purchases concentrate in San Salvador, La Libertad, and San Miguel, with the United States as the leading payment destination. Presented at the First Regional Workshop on Measuring Digital Trade for Arab Countries (Abu Dhabi) and at UNCTAD's E-Commerce Working Group (Geneva), where the methodology is now referenced as regional best practice.

Impact of Family Remittances on the Economic Growth of El Salvador: An Analysis of Their Productive Use
Impacto de las remesas familiares en el crecimiento económico de El Salvador: un análisis de su uso productivo
Working Paper · Central Reserve Bank of El Salvador
Co-authored study examining the short and long run relationship between remittance inflows and GDP growth using a Vector Error Correction (VEC) model within an extended Cobb-Douglas production framework with GDP, capital stock, employment, and remittances. Following Johansen cointegration tests confirming a stable long-run relationship, the long-run elasticity of GDP with respect to remittances is 0.13%, with an error correction coefficient of −0.66 (66% annual adjustment speed). The model explains 70.6% of GDP variation. Results indicate that productive channelling of remittances, particularly into human capital and investment, represents a pathway through which their aggregate growth impact could be amplified.

Estimation of Female Sectoral Employment and Wages in El Salvador, 1990 to 2015: An Input-Output Approach
Estimación del empleo y salario sectorial femenino en El Salvador, 1990 a 2015: un enfoque desde el insumo-producto
Undergraduate Thesis · Universidad Centroamericana José Simeón Cañas · October 2020
With Sofía Hernández Palacios, Zayda Ortiz Batres, and Ana Laura Pérez Gómez. Advisor: Dr. Mario César Sánchez Pérez.
Analyzes gender-based occupational segregation in El Salvador across 45 economic sectors over 1990 to 2015, using Wiedmann's Total Impact Multipliers methodology applied to the Leontief Input-Output Matrix. The TIMs approach expands employment and wage data from 14 aggregated sectors to 45 disaggregated branches, enabling granular analysis of labour market structure. Drawing on EHPM household-survey microdata, findings reveal persistent horizontal segregation: women remain concentrated in fewer than 5 of 45 sectors throughout the period. Documents structural gender wage gaps across educational levels and sectoral branches in Central American labour markets.
